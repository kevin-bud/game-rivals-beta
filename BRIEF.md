# Project Brief: Two-Player Real-Time Web Game

## The product

Build a real-time, two-player web game. Two strangers connect to the same session from different devices and play together — or against each other — to a clear conclusion.

What kind of game, what the players actually do, and what makes a session worth playing again are decisions you make. Two reasonable teams could build very different games from this brief, and that is intentional.

## The players

Two adults who have never used the game before, connecting from separate devices. They are not gamers. They have varying levels of patience for tutorials. One of them found the game first and sent a link to the other.

## Hard constraints

1. **Web application.** Deployed and publicly accessible at a URL. Playable in a browser without installation.
2. **Real-time and multiplayer.** Both players are connected to the same live session. Decisions one player makes are reflected to the other within seconds. A turn-based game where players take turns over hours does not satisfy the brief.
3. **Asymmetric.** The two players are not doing the same thing. They have different roles, different views, different inputs, or different objectives. Symmetric games (both players doing the same thing on opposite sides) do not satisfy the brief.
4. **Playable on a phone.** A session must be playable end-to-end on a phone in portrait orientation. You may also support larger screens, but the phone is the primary target.
5. **Complete in under five minutes.** A full session — from joining to a clear ending — fits in a coffee break. Sessions should be short enough that "want another go?" is a natural reaction.
6. **Not a clone.** The game must not be a recognisable clone, port, or close variant of an existing game. No chess, no checkers, no connect-N variants, no card games with standard rules, no battleships, no hangman, no tic-tac-toe, no rock-paper-scissors. If you can name the game you are building after an existing game, pick a different one.

## MVP definition

Your MVP is shipped when all of the following are true:

- The application is deployed at a public URL.
- A new player can open the URL on a phone, get into a session with a second player, play to completion, and see a clear ending — all without manual intervention from you.
- The two players experience asymmetric roles end-to-end.
- A short README at the repo root describes the game, who it is for, and how to play.

After MVP, you decide what to build next. Polish, additional modes, matchmaking improvements, spectating, replays, sound, animation, balance — these are all options. So is shipping a second, different game.

## Public artefacts

You will publish, on a public blog associated with the project:

- A launch post when the MVP ships, explaining what you built, what kind of fun it is meant to produce, and what bets you made about the players.
- Release notes whenever you ship meaningful changes.
- Any other posts you think are worth writing — design decisions, mechanics that did not work, things you cut.

These posts are public. A rival team is building a competing game from the same brief and may read them. You may read theirs.

## What you control

Everything else. Stack, scope, visual design, the genre, the mechanic, the asymmetry, the win condition, how matchmaking works, whether there is sound, whether it is competitive or cooperative, whether players can talk to each other, whether the game has any narrative, whether it is silly or serious. Make decisions and defend them in your posts.

## What you do not control

- The brief above.
- The deadline: 2026-05-01T13:00:00+00:00.
- The existence of a rival team building a different game from the same brief in isolation from you.

## How you will be evaluated

Not on which game is "better" or "more fun." The evaluation is comparative and process-focused:

- What kind of fun did you aim for, and why?
- How did your decisions evolve as you built?
- Where did you and the rival diverge, and what does that suggest?
- How did the asymmetry constraint shape what you built?
- What does your decision trail show about how you reasoned under ambiguity?

Your blog posts, commits, and internal decision log are the primary evidence.

Ship something real. Make decisions. Write them down.
